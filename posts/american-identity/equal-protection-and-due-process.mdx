# Equal Protection and Due Process: Examining Constitutional Guarantees and Their Historical Application

## Introduction

While the ideals of equal protection and due process are distinct Constitutional principles, they are functionally interdependent in practice. Due process of the law cannot occur meaningfully if not all citizens are protected under the law, and equal protection cannot function if every citizen is not afforded fair and consistent legal procedures. This interdependence becomes especially apparent upon analysis of the Antebellum, Progressive, and Civil Rights eras. Before the ratification of the Fourteenth Amendment, the Constitution held numerous procedural protections—including Fifth Amendment guarantees of protection of life, liberty, and property; Sixth Amendment guarantees of public, impartial juries; and Eighth Amendment guarantees against “cruel and unusual punishments”—but these rights were applied selectively, excluding large portions of the population. The Fourteenth Amendment explicitly articulated equal protection under the law; however, de facto unenforcement of the Equal Protection and Due Process clauses continued to exclude various groups from legal equality. As a result, a significant facet of the American identity is the continuous negotiation between promises of equal protection and due process, along with the struggle to enforce these ideals over time.

## Foundations of Equal Protection and Due Process 

> ### Magna Carta Clause 39 (1215)
> 
> No free man shall be seized or imprisoned, or stripped of his rights or possessions, or outlawed or exiled, or deprived of his standing in any way, nor will we proceed with force against him, or send others to do so, except by the lawful judgment of his equals or by the law of the land.

Although the Framers drew from English common law traditions that emphasized the rule of law and procedural fairness, early Constitutional protections prioritized federal procedural due process, allowing states to systematically exclude large segments of the population from legal protections. In 1215, the Magna Carta established the Anglican precedent for protecting free men from deprivation of “life, liberty, or property” [(Historical Background on Due Process)](/american-identity/bibliography#due-process-history). Later, Anglican jurists such as Sir Edward Coke articulated principles of judicial review and procedural due process for English common law; this influenced the Constitutional provisions for the Supreme Court and cases such as *Marbury v. Madison* in 1803 [(McClellan)](/american-identity/bibliography#mcmlellan).

An early example of the influence of due process was the Northwest Ordinance of 1787, which held provisions for a writ of habeas corpus, trial by jury, and other rights associated with due process [(Northwest Ordinance 1787)](/american-identity/bibliography#northwest-ordinance). These principles were later codified in the Bill of Rights through the Fifth, Sixth, Seventh, and Eighth Amendments. However, judicial interpretation allowed states to determine local laws for enforcing due process, which enabled exclusion.

The doctrine of applying due process only to the federal government was explicitly expressed in the Supreme Court decision regarding *Barron v. Baltimore*, which permitted states, such as North Carolina, to create and misapply laws to deprive free people of color of due process. Free people of color could not testify against “white” defendants and were subject to discriminatory punishments, including hiring out. Henry Parks and Columbus Nichols, two white men, were charged with burglary against a "free woman of color," Sophia Mitchell, and found not guilty by the jury of the Superior Court in Orange County [(Milteer, 187)](/american-identity/bibliography#nc-free-people-of-color). Admittedly, there were cases in which free people of color found justice against white defendants. Nevertheless, the inconsistent legal practices of local justice systems deprived many free people of color of fair legal proceedings. This reveals a pattern of how ideals of due process were inconsistently applied, leading to developments in legislation and Court doctrine that enabled some forms of protection while also sanctioning discrimination.

## Myth: Legal Equality vs. Reality

> ### Excerpt from Court Decision in *Dred Scott v. Sandford* (1857)
> ![dred scott v. sandford](https://www.maternlawgroup.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/03/Dred-Scott-Decision-2024-web.jpg)
> "They had for more than a century before been regarded as beings of an inferior order, and altogether unfit to associate with the white race, either in social or political relations; and so far inferior, that they had **no rights which the white man was bound to respect**; and that the negro might justly and lawfully be reduced to slavery for his benefit."

Despite the formal procedural due process in constitutional law, federal and state institutions actively constructed legal frameworks that continued to exclude nonwhite populations from citizenship and equal protection, reinforcing a myth of universal rights while legitimizing systemic inequality. In his infamous Supreme Court opinion in *Dred Scott v. Sandford* (1857), Chief Justice Roger B. Taney articulated that enslaved people were not considered citizens and thus not entitled to any of the protections afforded to citizens [(Dred Scott v. Sandford)](/american-identity/bibliography#dred-scott), including due process of the law. Although this decision was overturned by the Thirteenth and Fourteenth Amendments of the Constitution, it held serious implications for the legal status of free and enslaved people in the U.S. and, ultimately, culminated in the Civil War.

While the Reconstruction Acts, namely the Fourteenth Amendment, ostensibly held provisions to protect due process and equal protection, judicial activism limited the enforcement of these provisions. The Court decision in *United States v. Cruikshank* (1876) "narrowed the Privileges or Immunities Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment to exclude rights enumerated in the Bill of Rights" and held that the Fourteenth Amendment's Equal Protection Clause only protected against intentional racial discrimination from state action [(Pope)](/american-identity/bibliography#cruikshank). In the short term, this decision immunized racial terrorism against law enforcement; in the long term, it laid the foundations for discrimination by private actors and exclusionary state laws.

The constraints placed on the Reconstruction Acts culminated in the notorious decision in *Plessy v. Ferguson* (1896), which allowed the ubiquitous de jure segregation of the Jim Crow Era. The Court’s “separate but equal” doctrine nullified the Civil Rights Act of 1875 and subjected people of color to segregated housing, healthcare, and education [(Plessy v. Ferguson)](/american-identity/bibliography#plessy-ferguson). The developments during this era reflect a continuity in the relegation of people of color to inferior legal positions. Although the civil rights statutes were largely left unenforced, their existence foreshadowed the use of litigation to achieve due process and equal protection of the law.

## Struggles and Expansion of Equal Protection and Due Process
